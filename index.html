The Failure of the High School Musical Message

there’s not a star in heaven that we can’t reach

Once upon a time, I believed I could be anything, but the world soon reinforced its foreign restrictions on how anything was defined and for whom. My peace has lied in this meditation: if men don’t get their rights from God, they get them from other men.

The High School Musical trilogy set the tone for my early childhood like many other gZ*. Watching it again, I realized these movies were addressing a lot of what I care about in 2026: how to live humanly, mapping the set of who you are, and rejecting categories assigned by the world.

When they arrive, they split back into their “worlds.” Though we don’t know what they’re looking for, Troy appears to search for a social circle to hang with, and Gabriella searches for a place to read. Now they are in place, society can work its magic: the karaoke host puts a spotlight on them and forces them to share the stage.

Western philosophy taught us to divorce our intellect and reason from feeling and intuition. I learned about the metaphor of the elephant and the rider by Jonathan Haidt at a dinner with Sasha Chapin.

Troy and Gabriella transcend categories as just two people singing a song. That’s the second lesson: a soul, the essence of your set, cannot exist in any totalizing category outside of human.
